Background: The Geopolitical Risk Index

To evaluate global instability quantitatively, we use the Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR). The GPR is a widely used economic and political indicator developed by Dario Caldara and Matteo Iacoviello that measures geopolitical tensions by analysing the frequency of specific risk-related terms in international newspaper coverage.

The index is constructed using a dictionary-based search algorithm that scans large databases of newspaper articles and calculates the share of articles containing combinations of words related to geopolitical conflict. This approach allows the index to capture the intensity of geopolitical risk over time in a consistent and data-driven way.

Components of the GPR index

The GPR index is composed of eight underlying categories representing different forms of geopolitical tension. These categories capture both potential threats and actual conflict events.

  • War Threats (Category 1) - references to the possibility of war or military confrontation.
  • Peace Threats (Category 2) - breakdowns in diplomacy or negotiations that threaten peace agreements.
  • Military Buildups (Category 3) - discussions of increased military spending or mobilisation.
  • Nuclear Threats (Category 4) - references to nuclear weapons, proliferation, or nuclear tensions.
  • Terror Threats (Category 5) - warnings or potential threats of terrorism.
  • Beginning of War (Category 6) - reports of conflicts officially beginning.
  • Escalation of War (Category 7) - events where existing conflicts intensify.
  • Terror Acts (Category 8) - actual terrorist attacks or incidents.

How the index is constructed

Each of these eight categories contributes to the overall GPR score. The categories are weighted using statistical models that estimate the relative importance of different types of geopolitical events in shaping global risk levels. These weights are derived from historical data and calibrated using econometric modelling techniques.

The resulting index provides a monthly time series of geopolitical risk levels, allowing researchers to analyse long-term trends and compare different historical periods using consistent data.

Preliminary methodology

Our approach is to treat the idea of a “golden era” as a measurable statistical claim rather than just a feeling. We begin with the monthly global Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR), clean and filter the data so it consistently covers 1985 to the present, then split the timeline into two eras: a pre-2020 baseline and a post-2020 recent period. From there, we apply a small set of connected analyses designed to answer slightly different versions of the same question: is the world now generally riskier, more unstable, more extreme, or structurally different from before?

Phase 1 pipeline (global)

The global methodology is built in layers. Instead of relying on a single summary statistic, we combine baseline level, volatility, extremes, and regime-shift analysis so the interpretation is more robust. Each method is answering a different part of the overall research question.

  • Data preparation: load the monthly GPR series, convert dates, clean invalid values, sort chronologically, and restrict the working dataset to 1985–present.
  • Era split: define 1985–2019 as the “golden era” and 2020–present as the recent comparison period.
  • Baseline level: compare the mean and median of each era with bootstrap confidence intervals.
  • Volatility: compare standard deviation and rolling 60-month instability over time.
  • Extremes: define high-risk months using golden-era percentile thresholds, then compare how often and how strongly those extremes appear later.
  • Trend shift: fit separate pre-2020 and post-2020 linear trends and test whether the slope changes significantly.
What the baseline test asks
Are ordinary months now riskier on average, or does today only feel worse because of a few giant spikes?
What the volatility test asks
Even if average risk were similar, has the system itself become more unstable and harder to predict?
What the extremes test asks
Are months that would have counted as unusually severe in the golden era becoming more common now?
What the trend shift asks
Does 2020 behave like a real structural breakpoint, where the long-term direction of the series changes?

How the methods work together

These methods are intentionally complementary. Mean and median test the baseline level, volatility checks instability, extreme-value analysis checks tail risk, and piecewise trend analysis checks whether the system’s direction changed. Taken together, they let us distinguish between a world that is merely noisy and a world that is genuinely becoming more dangerous in a deeper statistical sense.

Phase 3 expansion (countries)

After validating the pipeline globally, we extend the exact same framework to the 44 country-level GPR share series. That means each country gets the same cleaning steps, the same era split, and the same set of plots and summaries. This makes it possible to ask not only whether the world as a whole looks different, but also whether that shift is broadly distributed or concentrated in particular places.

These three charts give the first answer: the post-2020 period does not just look noisy it appears structurally higher and more fragile than the 1985–2019 baseline.

Global GPR monthly values + rolling trends

This is the broadest view of the story. The raw monthly series is spiky, but the rolling means make the underlying shift easier to see. Instead of only a few isolated shocks, the recent period appears to sit on a higher overall level of risk than the older baseline.

Distribution shift

This chart compares the overall shape of the golden-era and recent-era distributions. The key point is that the recent period is not just defined by one or two dramatic spikes the whole distribution has moved upward. That lines up with the statistics: the golden-era mean is 100.00 while the recent mean is 119.61, and the median rises from 90.61 to 114.91. In plain language, typical months after 2020 are riskier than typical months before it, which supports the idea that the change is broad rather than anecdotal.

Trend shift around 2020

This plot asks whether 2020 marks a genuine structural change rather than just another bump in a noisy series. Before 2020, the fitted slope is slightly negative at about -0.015 GPR per month, which is basically flat to gently declining. After 2020, it becomes strongly positive at about +1.011 GPR per month, and the bootstrap confidence interval for the slope difference excludes zero. So, even at a preliminary level, the evidence suggests the recent era is not only higher it is also moving in a more unstable direction.
