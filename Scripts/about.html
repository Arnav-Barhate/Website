Method structure

The workflow starts with one cleaned monthly time series, then builds upward into a structured interpretation framework.

1. Data collection

The core dataset used in this project is the Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR). The GPR is a monthly index that measures the intensity of geopolitical risk by tracking the frequency of newspaper articles discussing war, tensions, threats, and other geopolitical crises.

Primary global variable
GPR — the recent global geopolitical risk index used for the baseline global analysis.
Country expansion
GPRC_* — country-level geopolitical risk share variables for 44 countries.
Time resolution
Monthly observations, which makes the analysis suitable for long-term structural comparison rather than daily event tracking.

2. Data cleaning and preprocessing

Before analysis, the raw CSV was cleaned so that the time series could be treated consistently. The goal was to ensure each observation corresponded to a valid monthly value and that all analyses used the same filtered time range.

  • Date parsing: the month column was converted into datetime format.
  • Numeric conversion: GPR values were converted to numeric and invalid entries were removed.
  • Chronological ordering: the dataset was sorted from earliest to latest month.
  • Time filtering: only observations from January 1985 onwards were retained.

Let the monthly geopolitical risk value be represented as:

\[ G_t \]

where \(t\) denotes time measured in months.

The final cleaned dataset is therefore a time series:

\[ \{G_1, G_2, G_3, \dots, G_n\} \]

where \(n\) is the total number of monthly observations.

3. Era definitions

To make the comparison explicit, the timeline was split into two eras:

Golden era
1985–2019
This is used as the baseline reference period and represents the pre-2020 world.
Recent era
2020–present
This is the comparison period used to test whether the world has become structurally riskier.

This split was fixed before interpretation, which makes the framework more objective and avoids moving the breakpoint after already seeing the results.

4. Baseline risk level analysis

The first question is whether the general level of geopolitical risk is higher after 2020. Two summary statistics were used: the mean and the median.

\[ \mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} G_t \]

The mean measures the average level of risk, while the median measures the “typical month”. Using both makes it possible to separate a broad upward shift from a change driven only by rare spikes.

Mean
Answers whether the overall average level of geopolitical risk has increased.
Median
Answers whether the ordinary month is riskier, even if extreme spikes are ignored.

5. Bootstrap confidence intervals

Because the GPR time series is highly non-normal and contains large spikes, non-parametric bootstrap resampling was used instead of relying on normality assumptions.

For each bootstrap sample \(b\), a statistic \(T_b\) is computed from a resampled dataset:

\[ T_b = f(G_1^*, G_2^*, \dots, G_n^*) \]

where \(G_t^*\) denotes the resampled data and \(f\) is the statistic of interest, such as the mean, median, or standard deviation. Repeating this process many times produces an empirical distribution from which 95% confidence intervals can be estimated.

6. Volatility analysis

Baseline level alone does not capture instability. A system may have a similar average level but fluctuate much more violently. To capture this, volatility was measured using the standard deviation.

\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}(G_t-\mu)^2} \]

In addition to overall era-wise volatility, a rolling 60-month standard deviation was computed. This makes it possible to see how instability changes through time rather than compressing the entire period into one number.

7. Extreme event analysis

To identify unusually risky months, thresholds were defined using the golden-era distribution. In particular, the 90th and 95th percentile cutoffs were used as reference points.

\[ G_t \ge Q_{0.95}^{(\text{golden})} \]

If a monthly value exceeds this threshold, it is classified as an extreme month relative to the pre-2020 baseline. Two quantities were then measured: the frequency of such months, and the average exceedance above the threshold.

  • Extreme frequency: the proportion of months above the golden-era threshold.
  • Mean exceedance: the average amount by which extreme months exceed the threshold.

This is the most natural supporting figure here because it directly visualises how often months cross the golden-era 95th percentile threshold.

8. Piecewise trend / regime-shift analysis

To test whether 2020 corresponds to a genuine structural change, a piecewise linear trend model was fitted separately to the two eras.

\[ G_t = a + bt \]

Here, \(a\) is the intercept and \(b\) is the slope, which represents the average change in GPR per month. Separate slopes were estimated before and after 2020.

\[ \Delta b = b_{\text{recent}} - b_{\text{golden}} \]

A bootstrap confidence interval was then constructed for the slope difference \(\Delta b\). If the interval excludes zero, the change in slope is treated as evidence of a structural regime shift.

9. Interpretation framework

The final interpretation is not based on one plot alone. Instead, results are combined using a structured framework:

Mean ↑ and median ↑
Suggests a broad upward shift in baseline geopolitical risk.
Mean ↑ only
Suggests the increase is driven mainly by rare but intense extreme events.
Median ↑ only
Suggests ordinary months are riskier even if rare spikes are not the dominant cause.
Volatility / extremes ↑
Suggests the system is becoming more unstable, not just higher on average.

10. phase 2: country-by-country expansion

After completing the global Phase 1 pipeline, the same analysis structure was applied to each of the 44 country-level series. This created a country-by-country extension of the same framework rather than a completely different methodology.

  • Input series: one country share series at a time, such as GPRC_AUS, GPRC_USA, or GPRC_CHN.
  • Same pipeline: cleaning, era split, baseline comparison, volatility, extremes, and trend change.
  • Output: a separate result folder for each country containing plots and a text summary.

This extension makes it possible to compare whether the same structural shift appears globally and locally, rather than relying on a single aggregate story.

11. Current scope and next steps

The completed work so far includes:

  • Global Phase 1 analysis on the main GPR series.
  • Country-by-country phase 2 expansion using 44 country series.
  • Static and interactive graph generation for website integration.
  • A preliminary interpretation framework linking all outputs back to the main research question.

The next stages will extend the same methodology to additional GPR columns and refine how the results are displayed and compared across the site.
